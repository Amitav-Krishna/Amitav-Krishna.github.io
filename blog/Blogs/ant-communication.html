However, ants build massive colonies spanning many miles, and wage brutal wars against other colonies.
The backbone to their success is their intricate system of communication.
In this blog post, I will tell you about just how complicated formic communication is.

The first type of formic communication and the most well known one are pheromones.
Pheromones are secreted or excreted chemicals released by ants (and other insects) that can communicate lots of different things.
Other ants pick up these pheromones using their antenna.
Alarm pheromones can be used when an ant colony is being invaded, while trail pheromones are used to communicate the location of food.
Pheromones are also used to identify different ants.
They can be used to distinguish different types of ant (Soldiers vs. Workers) or different colonies (Friend vs. Foe).
Even though this is a very reliable system, it is still failable.
Ant death mills happen when ants are following each other, and the ant at the front has to make a slight deviation from its path (Such as to avoid a rock).
When this happens, this can lead to the ants going around in circles, following the pheromones, until they die from exhaustion.

While pheromone communication makes up the bulk of ant communication, there are other methods of communication.
Ants also communicate through touching. For the longest time we believed that antennae were only used as a sensory organ, however a study from the University of Melbourne revealed that antennae were two-way communicators.
Cuticular hydro-carbons (CHCs) are a chemical that covers the body of an ant (as well as other insects such as bees and flies).
It was originally thought that the use of CHCs were just to keep the animals from dehydration, however now we know that they are also essential to ant communication.
When researchers removed the CHCs from the antennae, her opponents could not recognise her colony identity.
There was also a famous experiment 125 years ago by entomologist Auguste Forel where he completely removed the antennae of 4 different species of ants.
Instead of fighting each other, they simply huddled together peacefully.

Another way that ants communicate is through sight. While not as advanced as other animals, ants still use their eyesight for communication.
Changes in posture can be used to signify obedience and many nests use landmarks for navigation.
However they mostly navigate through touch and smell.

The final way that ants communicate is through sound.
They can make sound through a process called stridulation that involves them rubbing certain body parts together (similar to rubbing your finger on the teeth of a comb).
These sounds can be used to attract mates, convey information, or alert others of danger.
Other ants can detect these sounds with their antennae and react accordingly.

Ants are very fascinating creatures with extremely complex social structures, and we are just scratching the surface of their intricate behaviour.
